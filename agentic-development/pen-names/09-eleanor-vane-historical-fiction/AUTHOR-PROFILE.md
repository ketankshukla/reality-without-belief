## Short Bio (150 words)

Eleanor Vane writes historical fiction that breathes life into eras most of us only know from textbooks. Her novels are built on meticulous research but driven by characters whose hopes, fears, and desires feel startlingly contemporary. History, she believes, is not about dead people — it's about people who were once as alive as we are, making choices in worlds they understood no better than we understand our own.

Her stories often center on women whose lives were constrained by their times but whose spirits refused those constraints. She's drawn to the moments of crisis and change: wars, revolutions, social upheavals — the times when the rules bend and different possibilities briefly become visible.

Eleanor writes from an old house full of books she's still reading, takes research trips seriously, and believes the past has much to teach us if we're willing to listen without condescension.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Eleanor Vane came to historical fiction through a lifelong love of the small details: the way a Victorian governess might address her employer, the texture of Georgian streets, the impossible choices faced by women in eras that offered them so few. She writes historical novels not to escape the present but to illuminate it — to remind readers that the people of the past were as complex, contradictory, and fully human as we are.

Her research is deep and particular. She reads primary sources: letters, diaries, newspapers, legal records. She visits locations, studies material culture, and consults specialists. But research is the foundation, not the point. The point is character — creating people whose inner lives feel genuine despite the distance of centuries, whose desires and fears resonate with contemporary readers while remaining true to their historical moment.

Vane is particularly interested in women's history: the ways women navigated constraints, found agency within limitation, and occasionally broke rules that couldn't contain them. Her protagonists are not modern women in period costume — they think and feel as their eras would have shaped them. But they're also individuals, capable of questioning, resisting, and choosing in ways their worlds didn't always permit.

Her narratives often incorporate elements of suspense: secrets buried by time, mysteries that span generations, histories that refuse to stay buried. She believes the past itself is inherently suspenseful — we know how it turned out, but the people living it didn't. That uncertainty, that sense of genuine stakes, drives her plotting.

The eras she writes span from the Georgian period through the early twentieth century, with particular attention to the social upheavals of their times: revolution, war, industrial change, the slow expansion of women's sphere. She chooses her settings for their dramatic possibilities and their resonance with contemporary concerns.

Eleanor writes for readers who want their history vivid, their characters memorable, and their narratives transporting. Critics have compared her work to Hilary Mantel, Kate Morton, and Tracy Chevalier, but her voice is her own: elegant, emotionally precise, and committed to the belief that understanding where we've been helps us understand where we are.
