## Short Bio (150 words)

Vera Sinclair writes psychological thrillers about ordinary lives with extraordinary secrets underneath. Her books are set in houses you'd recognize — neat gardens, clean kitchens, marriages that look fine from the outside — and her specialty is the slow, devastating revelation that something has been very wrong for a very long time.

She's particularly interested in the gap between what women say and what they know, between the stories we tell ourselves and the ones that are actually true. Her protagonists are never simply victims. They're complicit, complicated, self-deceiving, and occasionally ruthless — fully human in the way that makes them genuinely unpredictable.

Vera lives somewhere quiet, writes her most disturbing scenes in broad daylight (which she finds appropriately unsettling), and believes the best thrillers should make readers feel, on the final page, that they should have seen it coming all along.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Vera Sinclair started writing psychological thrillers because she kept reading them and noticing what they got wrong about women. The women in her books are not simply victims waiting for revelation. They are complicit, complicated, self-deceiving, occasionally ruthless — fully human in the way that makes them genuinely difficult to predict. Her plots emerge from psychology rather than mechanics: she builds her characters first, understands exactly what they want and what they fear, and then constructs scenarios that pit those drives against each other in the worst possible way.

Her domestic settings are not accidental. The house, the marriage, the neighborhood — these are arenas where women's lives play out in specific and pressurized ways. Sinclair is fascinated by the power dynamics embedded in those structures: who controls the space, who controls the narrative, who gets believed and who doesn't. Her thrillers are, beneath the twists and reveals, examinations of those questions.

The unreliable narrator is her signature tool, but she uses it precisely. Her protagonists don't lie to the reader arbitrarily — they lie because they're lying to themselves, because the truth is something they genuinely cannot face. When the final revelation comes, it reframes everything that came before, making the reader complicit in the self-deception they should have seen through.

Sinclair writes in the tradition of the best British psychological suspense: slow-burn, character-driven, with endings that feel both surprising and inevitable. She believes a great thriller should be re-readable — that knowing the twist should make the early chapters more interesting, not less, as the reader finally sees what was hidden in plain sight.

Her process is meticulous. She outlines extensively, plants clues early, and revises ruthlessly to ensure every scene serves multiple purposes. The surface story must be compelling enough to carry readers forward, but the real story — the one happening beneath the lies — must be equally coherent when finally revealed.

She writes for readers who want their thrillers smart, their women complex, and their endings earned. Critics have compared her work to Gillian Flynn, Ruth Ware, and Lisa Jewell, but her voice is distinctly her own: controlled, precise, and capable of devastating turns delivered in perfectly measured prose.

## Protagonist Archetype
- Women in danger (from partners, neighbors, their own choices)
- Unreliable in some way (memory, perception, honesty)
- Complex and flawed — never purely victim or villain
- Intelligent but blind to something crucial
- Must actively uncover truth, not passively receive it
