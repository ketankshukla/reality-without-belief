## Short Bio (150 words)

Jett Holloway writes urban fantasy about the things that live in the gaps between what we know and what we refuse to see. His cities are familiar — you'd recognize the streets — but beneath the surface, there's another world: one of magic, monsters, and the damaged, dangerous people who keep the two from colliding.

His protagonists don't wield chosen-one powers. They're practitioners, hunters, survivors — people who learned about the supernatural the hard way and never got to unlearn it. They solve problems with cleverness, violence, and the occasional questionable alliance. They make bad jokes at worse moments. And they absolutely do not have their lives together.

Jett writes from somewhere urban, keeps irregular hours, and has spent more time than is healthy researching folklore, demonology, and the floor plans of buildings that don't exist. His books are fast, violent, and darkly funny.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Jett Holloway discovered urban fantasy through the simple realization that cities are already supernatural. They're layered with history, built on forgotten foundations, filled with spaces no one enters and doors that shouldn't exist. All he had to do was imagine what might live in those spaces — and who might be unlucky enough to deal with it.

His urban fantasy sits at the action end of the genre. The magic in his books is real, dangerous, and never free — practitioners pay prices in blood, sanity, or worse. The monsters are genuinely monstrous, drawn from folklore, demonology, and his own imagination. And the people who navigate this hidden world are not heroes in any traditional sense. They're survivors, opportunists, and occasional professionals who've learned that the supernatural doesn't care about your feelings.

Holloway's protagonists tend to be competent rather than powerful. They solve problems through knowledge, preparation, and the willingness to fight dirty. They have complicated relationships with both the mundane and magical worlds. They make mistakes, hold grudges, and crack jokes when they probably shouldn't. They're not saving the world — they're trying to survive it.

His action sequences are visceral and tactical. Magic in his books follows rules, and his characters use those rules cleverly. Fights are costly — nobody walks away clean. But there's also humor woven throughout, the dark comedy of people who've seen too much and cope by refusing to take anything seriously.

The cities in his books feel real because they're built on real urban geography — the abandoned subway lines, the buildings with wrong addresses, the neighborhoods that gentrification forgot. He layers the supernatural onto existing infrastructure, creating a world that feels like it could be hiding just beneath the one you know.

Holloway writes for readers who want their urban fantasy fast, violent, and funny. His books have been compared to Jim Butcher, Kevin Hearne, and Benedict Jacka, but his voice is his own: more cynical, more streetwise, and unafraid to let his characters be genuinely difficult people. The supernatural in his books is dangerous — but so are the humans.
