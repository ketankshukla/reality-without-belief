## Short Bio (150 words)

Cole Harker writes military science fiction about the people who make impossible decisions under impossible pressure — and have to live with the consequences. His books are built around tactics, loyalty, command burden, and the brutal arithmetic of interstellar warfare. He's less interested in the technology of the future than in the psychology of the people operating it under fire.

Harker spent years studying real military history — logistics failures, command breakdowns, the gap between orders issued and orders executed — before deciding that space was the right canvas for those stories. His battles feel real because they're built on real principles: the importance of supply lines, the fog of war, the way good people make terrible calls when the information is incomplete.

He writes from the Pacific Northwest, keeps odd hours, and his browser history is mostly declassified naval battle reports and orbital mechanics calculators.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Cole Harker came to military science fiction through the back door of history. Long before he was writing about fleet admirals and contested star systems, he was reading about Midway, the Falklands, Gallipoli, and a dozen other engagements where the outcome turned on decisions made by tired people with incomplete information. What fascinated him was never the hardware — it was the human element. The cascade of consequences from a single order. The way institutional culture determines who survives. The terrible math of acceptable losses.

His novels translate that fascination into the far future. The science in his books is hard enough to feel credible without requiring an engineering degree to follow. The tactics are grounded in real military thinking — Harker is a firm believer that space combat, if it ever happens, will look far more like submarine warfare than fighter dogfights. His command structures are realistic, his logistics matter, and his battles have consequences that echo through the rest of the narrative.

Harker's protagonists are rarely chosen ones. They're the competent ones — the officers who make the best of bad orders, the specialists who improvise when plans fall apart, the commanders who hold positions not for glory but because the people behind them need time. They're flawed, exhausted, and occasionally wrong. They make calls that haunt them. They win battles that cost more than they should and lose battles that shouldn't have been fought at all.

His writing philosophy centers on authenticity. The future he writes may be fictional, but the military culture, the command dynamics, and the weight of responsibility feel genuine. He spends weeks researching before writing a single battle sequence, ensuring that every tactical decision has logical foundations. His readers — many of them veterans or active military — have noted that his books "feel right" in ways that most sci-fi doesn't.

Harker writes for readers who want the future to feel earned, not handed out by convenient technology. His space is cold, his battles are costly, and his victories are never clean. He believes that the best military fiction is ultimately about people — about duty, loyalty, fear, and the choices we make when everything is on the line.
