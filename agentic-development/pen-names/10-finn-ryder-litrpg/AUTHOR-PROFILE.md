## Short Bio (150 words)

Finn Ryder writes LitRPG and progression fantasy for readers who appreciate the crunch. His books feature systems that make sense, progression that feels earned, and protagonists who level up through strategy, not destiny. He believes the best LitRPG should satisfy both the story brain and the gamer brain — compelling narratives wrapped around satisfying mechanical progression.

His protagonists aren't overpowered from chapter one. They start weak, think hard, and grind smart. They break systems through understanding, not arbitrary power-ups. And they face challenges that require more than just being higher level than the enemy.

Finn has been gaming since before he could read and writing since shortly after. He has opinions about skill trees, strong feelings about crafting systems, and a spreadsheet for every book he's ever written. He believes that in fiction, as in games, the journey from weak to strong is the whole point.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Finn Ryder started writing LitRPG because he couldn't find enough books that scratched the specific itch he was looking for: progression fantasy where the systems actually mattered, where leveling up required thought, and where the protagonist's growth felt genuinely earned rather than handed out by narrative convenience.

His approach to the genre is systematic. Before writing a book, he designs the magic system, the progression mechanics, and the world's rules — treating them as he would a game he was developing. Stats need to mean something. Skills need tradeoffs. Power spikes need to emerge from strategic choices, not random events. The result is fiction that satisfies readers who care about the crunch while still delivering compelling characters and narratives.

Ryder's protagonists are not chosen ones gifted with broken abilities. They start weak — sometimes pathetically so — and have to think their way up. They optimize builds, exploit synergies, and take calculated risks. They fail, learn, and adapt. Their victories come from understanding how the system works and using that knowledge creatively, not from being arbitrarily more powerful than their enemies.

But mechanical progression alone isn't enough. Ryder writes characters who change as they level, whose power creates new problems as it solves old ones, and whose relationships evolve alongside their abilities. His stories ask what it means to become stronger — and whether strength alone is ever the answer.

The worlds in his books range from full immersion VRMMOs to portal fantasies to entirely native progression systems. What unites them is coherence: the rules are clear, the stakes are real, and the systems interact in ways that reward careful readers. Easter eggs are planted for those who pay attention to the numbers.

Ryder writes for the LitRPG community that wants both story and system — readers who appreciate character development but also want to see the stat blocks, who root for protagonists but also evaluate their build choices. His books have been compared to the strategic depth of Andrew Rowe, the satisfying crunch of Shirtaloon, and the immersive worldbuilding of Travis Bagwell.

He games daily, reads voraciously, and maintains that theorycraft is a legitimate form of literary analysis. His Discord is active, his patch notes are detailed, and he genuinely wants to hear your thoughts on the meta.
