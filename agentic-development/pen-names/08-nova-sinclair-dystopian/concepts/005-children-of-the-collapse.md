# Children of the Collapse

## Author
**Nova Sinclair** — Dystopian Fiction

---

## Genre
Dystopian / Post-Apocalyptic / Coming of Age

---

## Concept

The adults remember Before. They talk about electricity, supermarkets, the internet, doctors who could fix anything. They mourn what was lost. They're paralyzed by grief for a world the children have never known.

Sixteen-year-old Kai doesn't understand their mourning. This world — the settlements, the scavenging, the careful cultivation of rooftop gardens and rain catchers — is the only world he's ever known. He's good at it. His generation is good at it. They're not broken by what happened because they have nothing to compare it to.

When a sickness sweeps through the adult population of Haven Settlement, Kai and the other young people are left to manage everything. At first, they expect the adults to recover and take back control. But the sickness lingers, and the children realize something: they're better at this than their parents ever were.

They don't waste time mourning. They don't make decisions based on "how things used to be done." They adapt, innovate, build. Under their leadership, Haven doesn't just survive — it thrives.

But the adults are recovering. And they expect to resume control of a settlement that's changed fundamentally in their absence. The children have to decide: hand back power to people who can't let go of the past, or complete the transition their illness started.

---

## Key Themes
- Generational difference in facing catastrophe
- The burden of memory vs. freedom of ignorance
- Youth competence when adults fail
- Power transitions

---

## Tone
Hopeful dystopian, generational conflict, survival competence.

---

## Unique Angle
The children are BETTER at the new world. They're not traumatized by what's lost.

---

## Target Audience
YA crossover readers, dystopian fans, readers interested in generational themes.

---

## Suggested Chapter Structure (10 chapters)

1. **Haven** — The settlement, the adults, the children
2. **The Sickness** — Adults fall ill
3. **Taking Over** — Young people assume responsibility
4. **Learning Fast** — Mistakes and adaptation
5. **Thriving** — Settlement improving
6. **Recovery** — Adults getting better
7. **The Conflict** — Who leads now?
8. **The Choice** — What kind of community they want
9. **The Transition** — Negotiating power
10. **Children of the Collapse** — Resolution
