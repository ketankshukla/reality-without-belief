## Short Bio (150 words)

Nova Sinclair writes dystopian and post-apocalyptic fiction about what happens after everything falls apart — and the people who have to decide what kind of world comes next. Her books are set in futures that feel disturbingly plausible: collapsed systems, scarce resources, communities trying to rebuild while others try to control.

Her protagonists aren't chosen ones or natural-born leaders. They're ordinary people making impossible choices — some that will haunt them, others that will define them. She's interested in the moral complexity of survival: what you're willing to do, what you can't forgive yourself for, and whether it's possible to remain human when humanity feels like a luxury.

Nova writes from somewhere that gets cold in winter, thinks too much about supply chains, and believes the best dystopian fiction holds a mirror to the present as much as the future.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Nova Sinclair started writing dystopian fiction because she couldn't stop thinking about systems — specifically, how fragile they are. The supply chains that keep cities fed, the infrastructure that keeps water clean, the social contracts that keep strangers from killing each other for resources. Her fiction emerges from the question: what happens when those systems fail?

Her post-apocalyptic worlds are built on plausible collapses. Climate catastrophe, pandemic aftermath, social breakdown, technological collapse — she researches the realistic mechanics of how civilizations end and what might grow in the ruins. Her futures feel possible because they're extrapolated from present vulnerabilities.

But Sinclair isn't primarily interested in the apocalypse itself. She's interested in the aftermath — in the communities that form, the power structures that emerge, and the moral compromises survival demands. Her books ask hard questions: What kind of person do you become when resources are scarce? What systems of control are justified by necessity? When does protection become oppression?

Her protagonists navigate these questions without easy answers. They're not action heroes or rebellion leaders by nature — they're people who end up in impossible situations and have to decide what they're willing to do. Some make choices they can live with. Others don't. The tension in her books comes from the gap between who her characters were before and who survival requires them to become.

Sinclair's prose is spare and controlled, matching her settings. She doesn't waste words on comfort. Her action sequences are brutal and consequential — resources are scarce, injuries matter, and death is permanent. But she also finds moments of connection, hope, and even dark humor in her collapsed worlds. Humans remain human, even when everything else has changed.

She writes for readers who want their dystopia realistic, their characters complex, and their endings earned. Her books have been compared to Cormac McCarthy's The Road, Emily St. John Mandel's Station Eleven, and the survivalist precision of Hugh Howey — but her voice is distinctly her own: unflinching about costs, clear-eyed about human nature, and ultimately interested in what we might build after we've lost everything.
