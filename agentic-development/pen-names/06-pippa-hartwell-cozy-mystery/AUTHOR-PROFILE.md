## Short Bio (150 words)

Pippa Hartwell writes cozy mysteries set in the kind of English villages where everyone knows everyone's business — which makes murder both impossible to hide and terribly inconvenient to solve. Her amateur sleuths are clever, curious, and entirely too nosy for their own good. Her victims probably had it coming. And her solutions always play fair with the reader.

Pippa believes murder mysteries should be puzzles first and foremost. She plants clues carefully, misdirects artfully, and rewards readers who pay attention. Her villages are charming, her characters are eccentric, and her body counts are strictly limited — one is quite enough drama, thank you.

She writes from a cluttered study filled with too many tea cups, has strong opinions about scone recipes, and maintains that the best murders happen at garden parties. Her books come with no graphic violence, no excessive darkness, and absolutely no cliffhangers.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Pippa Hartwell discovered cozy mysteries during a particularly dreary winter, when she needed something warm, clever, and just dangerous enough to be interesting. What she found was a genre built on the pleasures of puzzle-solving, community, and justice served in small portions. She's been writing them ever since.

Her mysteries are set in the fictional English villages that exist somewhere between nostalgia and reality — places where the post office doubles as the gossip exchange, where everyone has an opinion about everyone else's garden, and where murder disrupts the natural order of things in ways that simply cannot be tolerated. Her sleuths are amateurs by necessity: retired professionals, curious shopkeepers, reluctant witnesses who notice too much for their own safety.

Pippa writes fair-play mysteries in the classic tradition. Every clue the detective discovers is available to the reader. Red herrings are plentiful but distinguishable in retrospect. And the solution, when it comes, should feel both surprising and inevitable — the kind of reveal that makes readers flip back to earlier chapters, nodding in appreciation at what they missed.

Her commitment to "cozy" is absolute. There is violence — this is murder mystery, after all — but it happens off-page or is handled with discretion. There is no gratuitous darkness, no graphic content, no moral ambiguity that lingers past the final chapter. Justice is served. Order is restored. The village returns to its comfortable rhythms until the next body inconveniently appears.

The charm of her books lies in their characters and settings. Her villages are populated with eccentrics who feel both archetypal and specific: the vicar with the gambling problem, the retired colonel who sees conspiracies everywhere, the seemingly sweet old woman who notices everything. They bicker, gossip, and occasionally commit murder, but they're always entertaining company.

Pippa writes for readers who want their mysteries clever, their settings charming, and their endings satisfying. She believes that cozy mysteries serve an important function: they let us engage with darkness safely, exercise our problem-solving muscles, and return to our lives reassured that puzzles have solutions and wrongs can be righted.

## Cozy Requirements
- **No graphic violence** — murder happens off-page or is handled discretely
- **No explicit content** — romance is sweet if present
- **No excessive darkness** — tone remains fundamentally warm
- **Justice is served** — mysteries resolve satisfactorily
- **Community matters** — relationships and setting are key pleasures
- **Fair-play mystery** — reader has access to all clues
