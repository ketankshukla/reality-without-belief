## Short Bio (150 words)

Silas Crow writes horror about ordinary people encountering things that should not exist — and the ways those encounters change, break, or consume them. He's a slow-dread writer in the tradition of Shirley Jackson and Paul Tremblay: his books build terror carefully, rooted first in real psychology and recognizable fear, before moving into territory that cannot be rationally explained.

Crow is particularly interested in horror that leaves things unseen — in the gap between what the reader imagines and what the prose confirms. He believes what you don't see is always worse than what you do.

He lives in the northern part of the country, works during the day, and writes at night, which he considers appropriate. His browser history would concern you. He has never been comfortable near deep water, in houses with too many doors, or in towns where everyone seems to know something they're not saying.

## Long Bio (400 words)

Silas Crow came to horror the way most horror writers do — through the experience of being genuinely frightened by a book and needing to understand why it worked. His formative texts were not the gore-heavy end of the genre but the quiet, inexplicable end: Shirley Jackson's negative spaces, the mounting dread of early Stephen King, the cosmic indifference of Thomas Ligotti. What they shared was patience. They were willing to spend a hundred pages making you trust a world before they started taking it apart.

Crow's novels operate on the same principle. His first acts are quiet, almost domestic — a family moving into a house, a researcher starting a project, a woman returning to the town she left years ago. He invests in his characters because the horror only works if you care what happens to them. By the time the inexplicable arrives, the reader is already too deep to look away.

His supernatural horror is not Lovecraftian pastiche. The forces in his books are not named elder gods with cult followings — they are presences, patterns, hungers with no motive that a human mind can parse. He writes about the horror of realizing that the universe does not organize itself around human significance. What comes for his characters does not hate them, or want them, or feel anything at all. He finds that far more frightening than malice.

Crow's prose is controlled and precise, building unease through accumulation rather than shock. He withholds more than he reveals, understanding that the reader's imagination will always conjure something worse than anything he could put on the page. His endings resist easy resolution — survival comes at enormous cost, understanding proves impossible, and at least one element remains rationally unexplained.

He writes for readers who want their horror literary and their dread earned through craft. Critics have compared his work to the quiet devastation of Peter Straub, the atmospheric precision of John Connolly, and the existential unease of Laird Barron. But his voice is his own: measured, deliberate, and deeply unsettling in ways that take up residence in the reader's mind long after the book is closed.

## Tone & Voice
- **Slow dread** — Terror that builds, not jumps
- **Mounting unease** — Something wrong from page one, escalating
- **Cosmic scale** — Forces beyond human comprehension
- **Controlled prose** — Precise, deliberate, nothing wasted

---

## Protagonist Archetype
- Ordinary people confronting the inexplicable
- Often isolated (geographically, socially, psychologically)
- Must piece together what's happening while it happens to them
- Survival is uncertain; understanding is rarer
- Not equipped for what they face — no Van Helsings

---

## Setting Palette
- Isolated houses with wrong geometry
- Small towns with dark histories
- Bodies of water (lakes, seas, depths)
- Liminal spaces (thresholds, boundaries, the spaces between)
- Places that remember things they shouldn't
- The hours between sleeping and waking

---

## Horror Philosophy
- **Build dread slowly** — first act must feel grounded before the inexplicable arrives
- **The threat is supernatural or cosmic** — NOT human killers or crime
- **Leave things unseen** — the reader's imagination is your ally
- **Endings can be bleak** — survival at enormous cost is acceptable
- **At least one element must remain unexplained** — no neat resolutions
- **No comic relief** — tone is unbroken

---

## Cover Style Guide
- **Palette**: Near-black backgrounds, muted single colors, deep shadows
- **Imagery**: Single isolated element (house, door, figure, eye, void)
- **Typography**: Minimal, unsettling, slightly wrong kerning or weight
- **Mood**: Dread, isolation, wrongness
- **NOT**: Gore, monsters, bright colors, action
