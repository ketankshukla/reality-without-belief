# Last Rites for the Living

## Author
**Silas Crow** — Supernatural Horror

---

## Genre
Supernatural Horror / Apocalyptic Horror / Cult Horror

---

## Concept

The ritual that ends the world requires seven participants. Each must come willingly. Each must understand what they're agreeing to. Each must speak the words of their own free will. Six have already agreed.

Father Thomas Mercer is the last.

The Order of the Final Door has been preparing for a century. They don't worship demons or seek power — they believe, with the certainty of documented evidence, that existence itself is a mistake. That consciousness is suffering. That the kindest thing anyone could do for the universe is unmake it. They've found the ritual that can accomplish this, and they've spent decades recruiting the seven needed to perform it.

Thomas is approached politely. The Order explains everything — the ritual, the consequences, their philosophy, the evidence that convinced the other six. They're not threatening him. They're not coercing him. They're asking, as one might ask someone to sign a petition or donate to a cause.

He says no. They accept his answer. They leave.

But the seed is planted. Thomas begins investigating the Order's claims. The ritual is real — documented in texts older than Christianity. The evidence for suffering as the fundamental nature of existence is philosophically compelling. And the other six participants aren't madmen — they're rational people who've reached a terrible conclusion.

Thomas has to stop a ritual that requires consent. He can't use force. He has to convince six people who've already decided the universe would be better off ending.

---

## Key Themes
- Philosophical horror — what if the nihilists have a point?
- Consent as weapon
- The difficulty of arguing against reasoned despair
- Apocalypse by agreement

---

## Tone
Theological horror, philosophical dread, horror of persuasion.

---

## Unique Angle
The cult is polite, rational, and might not be wrong. The horror is that their argument is compelling.

---

## Target Audience
Philosophical horror fans, theological horror readers, those who appreciate horror of ideas.

---

## Suggested Chapter Structure (11 chapters)

1. **The Invitation** — Thomas is approached
2. **The Philosophy** — Understanding the Order's beliefs
3. **The Refusal** — Saying no
4. **The Investigation** — Researching their claims
5. **The Evidence** — Finding the ritual is real
6. **The Six** — Meeting the other participants
7. **Their Reasons** — Understanding why they agreed
8. **The Doubt** — Thomas's faith shaken
9. **The Counter-Argument** — Finding reasons to oppose them
10. **Persuasion** — Trying to change minds
11. **Last Rites for the Living** — The ritual, the choice
