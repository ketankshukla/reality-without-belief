# The Weight of Small Things

## Genre
Literary Fiction / Interconnected Stories / Drama

## Concept

On a single Tuesday in October, a small ceramic bowl breaks in an antique shop in Portland, Oregon. This event — insignificant, unwitnessed, immediately cleaned up — sets in motion a chain of consequences that will alter seven lives across the city over the following year.

The shop owner, grieving her late husband, sees the broken bowl as a sign and makes a phone call she's been avoiding. A customer who witnesses the moment chooses a different gift than planned, changing the trajectory of a relationship. The person who accidentally broke the bowl, a delivery driver, is late to his next stop, missing an encounter that would have changed his life — and meeting someone else instead.

This novel follows seven storylines that brush against each other, diverge, and ultimately converge, tracing the invisible threads that connect strangers. It's a meditation on causality, chance, and the way our lives are shaped by events we never even notice — the weight of small things that don't feel heavy until we see them from above.

## Key Themes
- The interconnectedness of strangers
- Chaos theory and human lives
- Grief, healing, and moving forward
- The moments we don't notice changing everything
- Coincidence vs. fate vs. pattern
- Finding meaning in random connection

## Tone
Quiet, observant, gradually building emotional resonance. The prose should feel precise and noticing — the kind of attention that makes small details feel significant.

## Unique Angle
Unlike typical interconnected-story novels, this one centers on an event so small it's almost nothing — making the point that every moment is potentially the breaking bowl.

## Target Audience
Readers of literary fiction and interconnected narratives. Fans of Elizabeth Strout, Ann Patchett, and the film "Crash." Book clubs. Adults who appreciate quiet, character-driven storytelling.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (21 chapters, three per character plus opener/closer)
1. The bowl — the breaking moment
2-4. Elena (shop owner) — grief, the call, the opening
5-7. Marcus (delivery driver) — the missed encounter, the new one
8-10. Lily (customer) — the changed gift, the changed relationship
11-13. James (Lily's recipient) — what the gift meant, what it led to
14-16. Sarita (Elena's phone call) — old connection renewed
17-19. David (Marcus's new encounter) — unexpected friendship
20. The convergence — all seven in one space
21. One year later — the weight revealed

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Autumn Portland — rain grays, fallen leaf oranges, coffee browns, ceramic blues
- Cover concept: A broken bowl with gold repair (kintsugi), or seven threads connecting across a city map
- Typography: Elegant, literary serif — understated and careful
