# The Dream Auditor

## Genre
Noir / Science Fiction / Psychological Thriller

## Concept

In a world where dreams can be recorded, stored, and used as legal evidence, Marcus Cole works as a Dream Auditor — a licensed investigator who reviews people's dreams to uncover fraud, infidelity, and hidden crimes. Dreams don't lie, the courts have ruled. The subconscious reveals what the waking mind conceals.

Marcus is good at his job, maybe too good. He's testified in hundreds of cases, sent people to prison based on what they dreamed. But he's started noticing anomalies: dreams that feel planted, memories that seem manufactured, subconscious footage that's too perfect, too convenient. Someone is hacking dreams — not to steal secrets, but to frame innocent people.

When Marcus is hired to audit the dreams of a death row inmate who claims she was framed, he discovers a conspiracy that reaches into the highest levels of the justice system. Dream evidence has been corrupted for years. The innocent have been convicted. The guilty have gone free. And the people behind it have just discovered that Marcus knows — which means they're about to audit his dreams too, and plant whatever evidence they need to destroy him.

## Key Themes
- The reliability of subjective experience as evidence
- Privacy of thought and the last frontier of surveillance
- Justice systems corrupted by technology
- The difference between memory, dream, and truth
- Noir themes of corruption and the lone investigator
- What happens when your own mind can be used against you

## Tone
Classic noir updated for the digital age. Hard-boiled narration, rain-slicked streets, moral ambiguity — but with neural interfaces and dream recordings. Paranoid, atmospheric, with flashes of dark humor.

## Unique Angle
This takes the noir detective and updates them for a world where the mystery isn't just "whodunit" but "what's real." When dreams can be fabricated, the very concept of truth becomes unstable.

## Target Audience
Fans of noir fiction and science fiction crossovers. Readers of Philip K. Dick, Raymond Chandler, and films like Blade Runner and Minority Report. Adults who enjoy smart, atmospheric mysteries.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (13 chapters)
1. The audit — Marcus reviews a cheating spouse's dreams
2. The death row case — Sarah Chen, convicted of murder
3. The anomaly — something wrong in her dream evidence
4. The pattern — Marcus reviews other cases, finds more
5. The dreamer underground — people hiding from audits
6. Following the trail — who has access to dream editing
7. The frame-up — Marcus realizes the scope of corruption
8. His own dreams — Marcus audits himself, finds tampering
9. Going dark — attempting to disappear from the system
10. The client list — discovering who's been framed and freed
11. The architect — who built this system of corruption
12. The final audit — Marcus's plan to expose everything
13. Waking up — aftermath and uncertain redemption

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Noir classics — deep shadows, neon accents, rainy grays, dream-state purples
- Cover concept: A man's silhouette with dream imagery projected on his face, or an eye with a recording device reflected in it
- Typography: Bold, slightly retro noir style mixed with tech fonts
