# The Good Immigrant

## Genre
Literary Fiction / Immigration Drama / Social Commentary

## Concept

Dr. Adaora Okonkwo spent twenty years being the "good immigrant" — the model minority who worked twice as hard, never complained, assimilated perfectly, and succeeded beyond anyone's expectations. She's now Chief of Surgery at a major hospital, married to an American, mother of American children, holder of American citizenship. She is the success story politicians point to when they say immigration works.

Then her younger brother Chidi arrives from Nigeria, seeking asylum after being targeted for his political activism. Unlike Adaora, Chidi is angry, unapologetic, and refuses to perform gratitude. He calls out racism when he sees it, questions why his sister had to lose so much of herself to be accepted, and generally refuses to be a "good immigrant."

As Adaora navigates her brother's case through an immigration system she never had to experience from this side, she's forced to confront what her success actually cost her — the parts of herself she abandoned, the injustices she overlooked, the compromises she pretends were choices. Chidi's refusal to assimilate isn't ignorance; it's integrity. And Adaora must decide whether she's been an inspiration or a warning.

## Key Themes
- The costs of assimilation
- "Good immigrant" vs. "bad immigrant" narratives
- Model minority pressure and its damage
- Sibling dynamics across generations
- The immigration system from multiple positions
- What we sacrifice to belong

## Tone
Sharp, compassionate, occasionally angry. The prose should capture both Adaora's measured success and Chidi's righteous fire.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about a struggling immigrant — it's about a successful one realizing what her success required and whether it was worth it.

## Target Audience
Readers of immigration fiction and literary social commentary. Fans of Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, Brit Bennett, and anyone navigating belonging.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The model — Adaora's successful life
2. The arrival — Chidi comes to America
3. The contrast — two ways of being immigrant
4. The case — navigating asylum process
5. The past — leaving Nigeria, what Adaora gave up
6. The confrontation — Chidi challenges her
7. The system — seeing immigration from inside
8. The cost — what assimilation actually took
9. The family — her American children, their identity
10. The hearing — Chidi's asylum case
11. The choice — Adaora's moment of decision
12. The public — speaking out or staying safe
13. The outcome — what happens to Chidi
14. The integration — not assimilation, something else

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Nigerian greens, American blues, passport document tones
- Cover concept: Two silhouettes on different paths that rejoin, or a passport with two photos
- Typography: Dignified, literary — suggesting serious fiction
