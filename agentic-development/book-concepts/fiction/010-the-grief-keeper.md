# The Grief Keeper

## Genre
Contemporary Fiction / Supernatural Drama

## Concept

Josephine "Jo" Mwangi has an ability she's never told anyone about: she can absorb grief. When she touches someone in mourning, she can take their pain into herself — not eliminate it, but carry it for them, give them respite. She discovered this at eight years old when she held her mother's hand at her father's funeral and felt something transfer, something heavy and dark that settled in her chest and never fully left.

Now forty-two, Jo works as a hospice counselor, secretly using her gift to ease the passage of the dying and comfort the bereaved. She's accumulated decades of grief that isn't hers — the losses of hundreds of strangers layered inside her like sediment. She functions, barely, through careful compartmentalization and a life deliberately kept small.

Everything changes when a young woman named Priya arrives at the hospice to say goodbye to her dying grandmother — and Jo realizes Priya has the same ability. For the first time, Jo has someone who understands. But Priya is reckless with her gift, absorbing trauma indiscriminately, and Jo sees her heading toward the same burnout that nearly destroyed her decades ago. To save Priya, Jo must finally confront the grief she's been carrying — including her own, which she's avoided processing by burying it beneath everyone else's pain.

## Key Themes
- The cost of empathy and emotional labor
- Carrying others' burdens vs. processing your own
- Mentorship and breaking cycles
- The difference between helping and hiding
- Grief as something to be experienced, not avoided
- What we lose when we give too much of ourselves

## Tone
Tender, melancholy, ultimately hopeful. The prose should feel weighted — the reader should sense Jo's accumulated burden — but also warm and human. Moments of connection and release.

## Unique Angle
This isn't a story about fixing grief — it's about the danger of trying to bypass it. The supernatural element serves as a metaphor for everyone who makes themselves responsible for others' emotions at the expense of their own.

## Target Audience
Readers of emotional literary fiction. Fans of Matt Haig, Fredrik Backman, and Elizabeth Strout. Anyone who has been a caregiver, worked in healthcare, or struggled with empathy burnout.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The hospice — Jo's daily work, secret revealed
2. The accumulation — decades of collected grief
3. Priya arrives — recognition of a kindred soul
4. The grandmother's passing — Priya's first major absorption
5. Jo's rules — the careful system she's built
6. Priya's recklessness — taking too much, too fast
7. The weight — Jo feels her own burden growing
8. Mentorship begins — trying to teach caution
9. The first grief — Jo's father, never processed
10. Crisis point — Priya nearly breaks
11. Confrontation — Jo must face her own avoidance
12. Release — learning to put grief down
13. Transmission — what Jo truly has to teach
14. The lighter chest — aftermath and continuation

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Soft grays, muted blues, touches of warm light breaking through
- Cover concept: Two hands touching with something luminous transferring between them, or a woman carrying a heavy shawl made of mist
- Typography: Gentle, approachable serif — warm and human
