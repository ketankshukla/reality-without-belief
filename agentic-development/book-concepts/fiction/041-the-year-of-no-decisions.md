# The Year of No Decisions

## Genre
Literary Fiction / Experimental / Contemporary

## Concept

After a lifetime of optimization — the right school, the right career, the right marriage, the right neighborhood — Catherine Lim has a breakdown at forty-five. Not a dramatic collapse, but a quiet paralysis. She realizes she's spent her entire life making "correct" choices and has no idea what she actually wants. Every decision was made to avoid bad outcomes, never to pursue desired ones.

Her therapist gives her a radical assignment: spend one year making no decisions. Don't choose where to eat — flip a coin. Don't choose what to watch — let someone else pick. Don't choose whether to accept invitations — say yes to everything. Remove the decision-making apparatus entirely and see what emerges.

What follows is a year of chaos, discovery, and liberation. Catherine finds herself at events she'd never have chosen, in conversations with people she'd have avoided, experiencing a life unfiltered by optimization. She hates some of it. She loves some of it. But for the first time, she's experiencing rather than managing. And by the end, she discovers something she's never had: actual preferences, born from experience rather than fear.

## Key Themes
- The tyranny of optimization
- Analysis paralysis and its costs
- Experience vs. management of life
- Finding authentic desire
- The freedom of surrendering control
- Decision fatigue in modern life

## Unique Angle
This isn't about spontaneity as obviously good — some of Catherine's no-decision choices are terrible. The point is that she finally has data about what she wants.

## Target Audience
Readers exhausted by self-optimization. Fans of literary fiction about modern life. Adults who feel they've been "choosing correctly" without living.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (12 chapters — one per month plus prologue/epilogue)
1. The paralysis — Catherine's breakdown
2. January — the first experiments
3. February — hating the randomness
4. March — first unexpected pleasure
5. April — relationships complicated
6. May — work implications
7. June — the family vacation she didn't choose
8. July — finding patterns in preferences
9. August — the rebellion (wanting to choose again)
10. September — understanding what she's learned
11. October-December — integration
12. The preference — Catherine's first authentic choice

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Decision-tree grays giving way to experiential color
- Cover concept: A coin in mid-flip, or a woman standing at a crossroads with her eyes closed
- Typography: Clean, modern — the aesthetic of contemporary fiction
