# The Algorithm of Us

## Genre
Romance / Science Fiction / Contemporary Fiction

## Concept

In 2032, the dating app Soulgorithm has achieved an 94% success rate for lasting marriages by using advanced AI analysis of everything: social media history, biometric responses, communication patterns, even micro-expressions during video calls. People who match are statistically guaranteed compatibility. Divorce rates have plummeted. The algorithm is never wrong.

Maya Chen and David Park are a perfect match — 99.7% compatibility, the highest in Soulgorithm's history. They meet, and it's everything the algorithm promised: easy conversation, shared values, mutual attraction, complementary quirks. Within six months, they're engaged. Their wedding will be broadcast as a Soulgorithm promotional event.

Then Maya meets someone at a coffee shop. Iris. No algorithm. No compatibility score. Just a conversation that turns into another, and another. Maya feels something with Iris that she's never felt with David — something chaotic, inexplicable, terrifying. Something the algorithm would have screened out because it can't be optimized.

Now Maya must choose: the statistically perfect life, or the unmeasurable one. And in a world where data-driven decisions have become synonymous with rational ones, choosing chaos feels like choosing madness.

## Key Themes
- Optimization vs. authenticity in human connection
- The quantification of love and its limitations
- Free will in an age of predictive algorithms
- What "compatibility" actually means
- The value of inefficiency, difficulty, and surprise
- LGBTQ+ identity and unexpected self-discovery

## Tone
Warm, questioning, romantic but intellectually curious. The prose should balance genuine romance with philosophical undertones. Neither path (algorithm vs. chaos) is presented as obviously correct.

## Unique Angle
This isn't anti-technology — David and the algorithmic match are genuinely good. The question isn't "is the algorithm wrong?" but "is being right enough?"

## Target Audience
Readers of smart romance and speculative fiction. Fans of Sally Rooney, Emily Henry, and Black Mirror. LGBTQ+ readers. Adults 25-45 interested in dating app culture critique.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (12 chapters)
1. The match — Maya and David's perfect meeting
2. The algorithm's promise — how Soulgorithm works
3. The engagement — everything proceeding perfectly
4. The coffee shop — Iris appears
5. Chaos theory — what Maya feels vs. what she should feel
6. David's perfection — why leaving would be irrational
7. Iris's world — outside the optimized life
8. The glitch — Maya investigates her own algorithm profile
9. Coming out (to herself) — identity crisis
10. The confession — Maya tells David the truth
11. The choice — algorithm or unknown
12. Unoptimized — aftermath and new beginning

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Soft app-interface pastels disrupted by warmer, messier watercolors
- Cover concept: Two perfectly matching puzzle pieces with a third irregular piece between them, or a heart-shaped data visualization fragmenting
- Typography: Modern, clean — like an app interface — with hand-drawn elements intruding
