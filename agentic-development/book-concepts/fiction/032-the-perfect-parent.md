# The Perfect Parent

## Genre
Domestic Thriller / Psychological Drama

## Concept

Margot Ellis is the mother every other mother envies. Her children are accomplished, polite, and thriving. Her home is organized without being sterile. Her social media showcases a family life that seems too perfect to be real — because it is. Margot has been performing perfect motherhood for fifteen years while hiding crippling anxiety, secret resentments, and a growing sense that she's disappeared entirely into the role.

Then her oldest child, seventeen-year-old Olivia, attempts suicide. Olivia, who seemed perfect. Olivia, who Margot designed to seem perfect. The note Olivia leaves doesn't blame her mother exactly, but it describes the suffocating pressure of living up to an image, the impossibility of being allowed to fail, the exhaustion of performing happiness.

In the aftermath, Margot must confront what her perfectionism cost her daughter — while the other mothers in her orbit begin experiencing their own cracks, their own children revealing the cost of the performance. What started as one family's crisis becomes an examination of what modern motherhood demands and what it destroys.

## Key Themes
- Perfectionism and its generational transmission
- The performance of good parenting vs. actual good parenting
- Social media's impact on family life
- Children as reflections of parental worth
- The permission to be imperfect
- Maternal identity and self-erasure

## Tone
Tense, introspective, ultimately compassionate. The prose should capture the exhaustion of maintaining perfection while building toward genuine release.

## Unique Angle
This isn't a critique of bad mothers — Margot genuinely loves her daughter. The horror is that good intentions and toxic perfectionism coexist.

## Target Audience
Parents, especially mothers, feeling overwhelmed by expectations. Fans of domestic fiction and psychological drama. Readers of Celeste Ng, Liane Moriarty, and similar.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The perfect morning — the performance in action
2. The call — Olivia is in the hospital
3. The note — what Olivia wrote
4. Fifteen years — how Margot built this life
5. The other mothers — watching their reactions
6. Margot's childhood — where her perfectionism began
7. Olivia's recovery — the slow conversations
8. The cracks spread — other families begin breaking
9. The group — mothers finally talking honestly
10. Margot's confession — admitting what she hid
11. Letting go — trying to release the performance
12. The relapse — perfectionism is hard to quit
13. Real recovery — Olivia and Margot together
14. Good enough — what that actually looks like

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Pinterest-perfect pastels revealing cracks and darker undertones
- Cover concept: A perfect family photo with one figure's smile slightly wrong, or a pristine home with a single visible crack
- Typography: Clean, aspirational — the aesthetic of lifestyle perfection
