# The Reputation Economy

## Genre
Dystopian Fiction / Social Satire / Thriller

## Concept

In 2038, traditional currency has been replaced by "reputation credits" — a social score that determines everything from where you can live to what jobs you qualify for to whether stores will serve you. Every interaction is rated. Every transaction reviewed. Your worth as a human being is a number visible to everyone you meet, updating in real-time based on algorithmic assessment of your social contributions, reliability, and "community impact."

Priya Sharma was a 4.8 — nearly perfect, a model citizen with access to the best neighborhoods, the best healthcare, the best life. Then she made a mistake: she publicly defended someone with a 2.1 rating, arguing they deserved a chance. Within 72 hours, her score dropped to 3.2. Doors that always opened now stayed closed. Friends stopped returning calls. Her apartment lease was terminated. She experienced the vertigo of social free-fall.

Now living in a 2.5-or-below district, Priya discovers an underground movement of "score hackers" — people who've figured out how to game the system, inflate ratings, and crash the scores of enemies. They offer to restore her reputation. But joining them means becoming complicit in a system she now sees as monstrous. Priya must decide: climb back up by any means necessary, or fight to destroy the entire hierarchy from below.

## Key Themes
- Social credit systems and their human cost
- The quantification of human worth
- Class mobility and its illusions
- Solidarity vs. individual advancement
- The performance of acceptability
- Who designs these systems and who benefits

## Tone
Satirical but grounded, increasingly angry. The prose should make the dystopian elements feel plausible — this is Black Mirror territory, extrapolation more than invention.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about a hero who was always rebellious — Priya benefited from the system and believed in it. Her radicalization is gradual and earned.

## Target Audience
Fans of dystopian fiction and social commentary. Readers of Dave Eggers, George Orwell, and Naomi Alderman. Adults concerned about social media, rating systems, and algorithmic control.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The 4.8 life — Priya at the top
2. The defense — standing up for a low-scorer
3. The fall — watching her number drop
4. 3.2 territory — learning what mid-score life is like
5. The district — being forced into low-score housing
6. The underground — meeting the score hackers
7. How it works — learning the system's vulnerabilities
8. The offer — restore your score for a price
9. Complicity — participating in score manipulation
10. The victims — seeing who gets hurt by hacking
11. The architects — discovering who built the system
12. The choice — individual escape or collective action
13. The uprising — attempting to crash the system
14. The aftermath — what remains when scores fail

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Corporate clean blues, rating-star golds, warning reds, poverty grays
- Cover concept: A face with a rating score hovering above it, or a smartphone screen showing a plummeting graph
- Typography: Modern, app-like, corporate-friendly — the aesthetic of surveillance
