# The Zero Footprint

## Genre
Eco-Thriller / Near-Future Fiction / Moral Drama

## Concept

Dr. Yuki Tanaka invented the technology that saved the world: carbon capture so efficient that humanity achieved net-zero emissions in a decade. She won the Nobel Prize. She's called the savior of civilization. But Yuki has been hiding something: the captured carbon has to go somewhere, and she discovered too late that the storage sites are unstable. Within fifty years, the carbon will release catastrophically — a delayed apocalypse she accidentally engineered.

Now seventy-two, Yuki has spent two decades secretly searching for a solution while publicly accepting accolades for the technology that may still doom humanity. She's found one possibility: a modification that could stabilize the storage, but implementing it requires confessing what she knew and when she knew it. Her reputation, her legacy, possibly her freedom — all would be destroyed.

When a young climate scientist begins noticing the same instabilities Yuki found decades ago, Yuki must decide: let the younger scientist take the fall for "discovering" the problem, or confess everything and try to lead the solution herself. Either way, the world that celebrated her will have to reckon with the truth: even the people who save us can make catastrophic mistakes.

## Key Themes
- The burden of scientific success
- Cover-ups born from shame rather than malice
- Intergenerational climate responsibility
- Whether redemption is possible at global scale
- The complexity of heroism
- Confession vs. managed revelation

## Tone
Weighty, morally complex, ultimately hopeful about human capacity for repair. The prose should feel like important testimony.

## Unique Angle
The climate hero as tragic figure — not a villain, not an innocent, but a person who made a terrible mistake while trying to save everyone.

## Target Audience
Readers of climate fiction and moral thrillers. Fans of Kim Stanley Robinson, Richard Powers, and literary science fiction.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The hero — Yuki receiving another award
2. The discovery — finding the instability twenty years ago
3. The young scientist — Dr. Maya Chen notices something
4. The cover-up — how Yuki rationalized silence
5. Parallel investigations — past and present
6. The calculation — what confession would cost
7. Maya gets closer — the truth emerging
8. The choice point — stop Maya or help her?
9. The confession — Yuki tells the truth
10. The fallout — public and personal consequences
11. The solution — can it actually be fixed?
12. The collaboration — Yuki and Maya working together
13. The implementation — attempting the repair
14. The legacy — what remains after truth

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Climate greens, carbon blacks, scientific whites, warning oranges
- Cover concept: A woman casting a long shadow over a globe, or carbon storage tanks with visible cracks
- Typography: Scientific, authoritative — suggesting peer-reviewed gravity
