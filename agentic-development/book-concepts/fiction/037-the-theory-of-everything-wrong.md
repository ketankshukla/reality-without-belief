# The Theory of Everything Wrong

## Genre
Literary Fiction / Academic Satire / Drama

## Concept

Dr. Samuel Okafor spent forty years developing a unified theory of physics that promised to explain everything — the nature of reality, consciousness, time, existence itself. His theory was beautiful, revolutionary, and wrong. He published it anyway, believing he'd find the error later. Instead, the theory caught fire. It was taught in universities, cited in thousands of papers, used as the foundation for experiments that seemed to confirm it. Samuel became the most celebrated physicist since Einstein.

Now eighty-two, with his Nobel Prize on the shelf and his legacy secure, Samuel has finally found the error. A small mathematical mistake on page 47 that invalidates everything. Forty years of physics built on a foundation that doesn't exist.

He could stay silent. He'll be dead soon anyway, and no one else has found the error. Or he could confess and watch his life's work — and the work of thousands of students and colleagues who built on his theory — crumble. This is the story of a man choosing between legacy and truth, between comfort and integrity, in the final chapter of his life.

## Key Themes
- Scientific integrity and the courage to be wrong
- Legacy vs. truth
- How errors propagate through systems of belief
- The academic incentive structure
- What we owe to those who trusted us
- Finding meaning in correction rather than achievement

## Tone
Intellectually engaging, morally weighty, ultimately redemptive. The prose should convey both the beauty of physics and the burden of conscience.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about a fraud — Samuel genuinely believed he was right. The question is what an honest person does when they discover honest error.

## Target Audience
Readers of literary fiction with intellectual settings. Fans of Richard Powers, science-focused novels, and academic satire. Adults interested in ethics and knowledge.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (13 chapters)
1. The discovery — finding the error at 82
2. The glory days — how the theory was celebrated
3. The mathematics — understanding what went wrong
4. The students — those who built careers on his work
5. The silence option — what happens if he says nothing
6. The confession draft — writing what he'd need to say
7. His wife — the person he tells first
8. The colleague — testing the waters
9. The institution — how science handles retraction
10. The decision — choosing truth
11. The announcement — going public
12. The aftermath — how people respond
13. The correction — what remains valuable
