# The Forgetting Game

## Genre
Domestic Thriller / Psychological Suspense

## Concept

Claire Ashford wakes up one morning to find that her husband of fifteen years doesn't remember her. Not amnesia from an accident — he simply has no memory that she exists. His phone has no photos of her. Their house shows no trace of her presence. Their daughter looks at Claire like a stranger. According to every record, every person, every piece of evidence, Claire has never been part of this family.

But Claire remembers everything. Fifteen years of marriage, raising their daughter, the fights, the reconciliations, their entire shared life. She has memories of a reality that apparently never existed.

As Claire desperately tries to prove she belongs, she begins to uncover disturbing possibilities: Either someone has orchestrated an elaborate gaslighting campaign of impossible scope, or her memories themselves are fabricated, and she's a stranger who somehow acquired false memories of this family. The deeper she investigates, the more she questions which reality is true — and whether the answer will destroy her or the family she believes is hers.

## Key Themes
- The fragility of identity based on memory
- Gaslighting and its extremes
- What makes someone "belong" to a family
- The unreliability of subjective experience
- Trust and betrayal in marriage
- The horror of being erased

## Tone
Paranoid, disorienting, increasingly desperate. The prose should make the reader feel Claire's uncertainty — never sure what's real, constantly questioning.

## Unique Angle
Unlike typical amnesia stories where the victim forgets, here everyone else has forgotten — making the protagonist's sanity the central question.

## Target Audience
Fans of domestic thrillers and psychological suspense. Readers of B.A. Paris, Ruth Ware, and Gillian Flynn. Adults who enjoy unreliable narrator stories.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The morning — waking up to non-recognition
2. Proof that doesn't exist — searching for evidence of her life
3. The husband's confusion — who is this woman?
4. The daughter's fear — stranger in the house
5. Claire's memories — are they real?
6. The investigation begins — hiring someone to find truth
7. The other woman — was there someone before Claire?
8. Cracks in the narrative — inconsistencies emerge
9. The institution — someone suggests Claire is disturbed
10. The photographs — finding evidence that changes everything
11. The conspiracy — or the delusion?
12. Confrontation — Claire forces the truth
13. The revelation — which reality is real?
14. The aftermath — living with the answer

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Domestic warmth invaded by clinical whites and cold grays
- Cover concept: A family photo with one figure erased or transparent, or a woman's reflection that doesn't match her
- Typography: Clean, modern, slightly unsettling — sans-serif that feels clinical
