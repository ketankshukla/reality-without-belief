# The Undo Button

## Genre
Science Fiction / Thriller / Moral Drama

## Concept

The technology is simple: a small device implanted behind the ear that allows you to "undo" the last 60 seconds of your life. Not time travel — reality revision. Make a mistake, hit undo, try again. Say the wrong thing, hit undo, say something better. Get hit by a car, hit undo, stay on the sidewalk. The device has revolutionized everything from surgery to sports to dating.

Nathan Cross is a professional "Undoer" — an elite consultant who helps wealthy clients navigate high-stakes situations by undoing their mistakes in real-time. A hostile business negotiation? Nathan's there, helping his client undo every misstep until they get the perfect outcome. A surgery? Nathan assists doctors who can undo the scalpel slips.

But Nathan has been keeping track of something troubling: every time someone undoes, someone else's life changes without their knowledge. The undo doesn't happen in isolation. When his client undoes a car accident, someone else gets hit instead. When a surgeon undoes a nick, a different patient develops an unexplained complication. The universe balances. And Nathan has personally been responsible for undoing outcomes that resulted in dozens of deaths no one will ever connect to him.

## Key Themes
- Consequences exported to strangers
- The illusion of consequence-free choice
- Privilege and who gets to avoid mistakes
- The butterfly effect in reverse
- Moral responsibility for indirect harm
- Whether some mistakes should stand

## Tone
Slick thriller surface with growing moral dread underneath. The prose should feel polished and confident like Nathan, then crack.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about the technology failing — it works exactly as designed. The horror is that working perfectly still causes harm.

## Target Audience
Fans of high-concept thrillers and Black Mirror. Readers of Blake Crouch and near-future speculation.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The professional — Nathan's elite work
2. The tool — how undo technology works
3. The pattern — noticing correlated events
4. The investigation — tracking the displaced consequences
5. The price — realizing people are dying
6. The client list — who's been using Nathan's services
7. The confrontation — telling his employer
8. The cover-up — they already knew
9. The whistleblower's dilemma — go public?
10. The undo attempt — can they undo the undos?
11. The cascade — consequences of revelation
12. The choice — Nathan's final decision
13. The exposure — truth goes public
14. The aftermath — a world that knows
