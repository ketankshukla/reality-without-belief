# The Deleted Scenes

## Genre
Thriller / Metafiction / Psychological Drama

## Concept

Oscar-winning screenwriter Julian Cross is dying of cancer at 67, with six months to live. His final wish is to write one more screenplay — but not fiction. He wants to write the true story of his life, the real version that contradicts the sanitized memoir he published twenty years ago. The affairs he denied. The collaborator whose work he stole. The accident that wasn't an accident. The lies that built his legend.

But as Julian begins writing, something strange happens: people start dying. His former writing partner, found dead hours after Julian wrote their confrontation scene. His ex-wife, killed in a manner identical to a scene he drafted but hadn't shared with anyone. Either someone is reading his work and committing murders to stop the truth from emerging, or something impossible is happening — the screenplay is coming true as he writes it.

Julian must finish his confession while navigating an impossible question: Is he writing about reality, or is his writing creating reality? And if his words have power, what happens when he writes his own death scene?

## Key Themes
- The power and responsibility of narrative
- Truth-telling as danger
- The stories we tell about ourselves vs. reality
- Creative guilt and stolen credit
- Mortality and the urgency of honesty
- Metafictional questions about authorship and agency

## Tone
Hitchcockian tension with literary self-awareness. The prose should feel cinematic — scene breaks, visual descriptions, dialogue-heavy — reflecting Julian's screenwriter perspective.

## Unique Angle
This blends thriller mechanics with metafictional play, asking whether writing is creation or discovery, and whether the stories we tell become true simply by being told convincingly enough.

## Target Audience
Fans of smart thrillers and metafiction. Readers of Paul Auster, Charlie Kaufman fans, anyone interested in the craft of storytelling. Hollywood observers and film enthusiasts.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (13 chapters)
1. The diagnosis — Julian decides to tell the truth
2. Act One begins — writing the real story
3. The first death — coincidence or consequence?
4. The investigation — police become involved
5. The collaborator's story — what Julian really did
6. The second death — pattern emerges
7. Impossible causation — Julian tests his theory
8. The ex-wife's chapter — secrets of the marriage
9. Who's reading? — searching for the killer
10. The accident — the truth Julian has avoided longest
11. The writer's trap — Julian can't stop writing
12. The final scene — writing his own ending
13. Credits roll — aftermath and ambiguity
