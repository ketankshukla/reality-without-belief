# The Last Analog Man

## Genre
Literary Fiction / Near-Future / Character Study

## Concept

Henry Ware is 68 years old and has never used a smartphone, never had an email address, never posted on social media, and has no digital footprint whatsoever. In 2035, this makes him an anomaly — possibly the last person in America who exists entirely in the physical world. He lives in a cabin in Vermont, pays cash for everything, reads physical newspapers, and writes letters by hand.

When a documentary filmmaker discovers Henry's existence, she becomes obsessed with understanding him. Not as a curiosity or a cautionary tale, but genuinely: How has he avoided what everyone else accepted as inevitable? What does he know that everyone else has forgotten? And is he happier, or just different?

As the documentary brings attention to Henry, he becomes an unlikely icon — celebrated by digital detox movements, vilified by tech companies, studied by academics. But the attention itself begins destroying what made his life possible. The more famous the analog man becomes, the more his analog existence becomes impossible. And Henry must decide: retreat further into isolation, or finally engage with a world he's spent decades avoiding.

## Key Themes
- What we've lost in the digital transition
- Solitude vs. isolation vs. loneliness
- The impossibility of opting out of systems
- Attention as invasion
- What "connection" actually means
- Whether the old ways were better or just different

## Tone
Meditative, wry, occasionally melancholy. Henry's voice should feel like a long conversation with someone who's been paying attention to different things than everyone else.

## Unique Angle
This isn't luddite propaganda — Henry isn't presented as obviously right. The question is genuine: what's the actual cost-benefit of how we live now?

## Target Audience
Readers questioning digital life. Fans of Wendell Berry, Richard Powers, and slow-paced literary fiction. Adults experiencing digital burnout or curious about alternatives.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (12 chapters)
1. The cabin — Henry's daily life
2. The filmmaker — Maya finds her subject
3. Fifty years unplugged — how Henry got here
4. The interview — first real conversation
5. The documentary — filming begins
6. Going viral — Henry becomes famous without knowing
7. The pilgrims — people seeking wisdom
8. The invasion — attention destroys peace
9. The offers — tech companies want him
10. The decision — how to respond to fame
11. The retreat — attempting to disappear again
12. What remains — Henry's final choice and wisdom

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Natural Vermont greens and browns, analog warmth, paper textures
- Cover concept: A man's silhouette against a forest, no technology visible, or an old typewriter in a modern wasteland
- Typography: Something that could be typewritten — Courier or similar — suggesting the analog world
