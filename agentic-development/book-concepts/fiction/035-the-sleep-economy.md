# The Sleep Economy

## Genre
Dystopian Fiction / Workplace Thriller / Social Commentary

## Concept

In 2041, a pharmaceutical breakthrough has made sleep optional. A simple pill eliminates the need for sleep with no side effects, effectively adding eight hours to every day. Employers now expect 16-hour workdays. Those who choose to sleep — who value dreams, rest, or simply prefer the old way — find themselves unemployable, considered lazy, falling behind.

Maya Torres was a successful architect who took the pill for three years, riding the wave of hyperproductivity, designing buildings during hours that used to be unconscious. Then she started having waking dreams — hallucinations that felt like the sleep she'd denied herself leaking through. She quit the pills, started sleeping again, and watched her career disintegrate.

Now Maya exists in the underground economy of "Sleepers" — people who've opted out of the 24-hour society. She works night shifts that no one else wants, lives in neighborhoods where the awake don't go, and organizes with others who believe that something essential is being lost in the endless day. When she discovers that the waking dreams aren't a bug but a feature — that the pharmaceutical company knew extended wakefulness causes psychological damage — she has evidence that could bring down the most profitable industry in history.

## Key Themes
- The commodification of all human time
- What rest provides beyond efficiency
- Class division through productivity expectations
- Dreams and their psychological necessity
- Corporate suppression of side effect data
- Opting out of systems as resistance

## Tone
Exhausting, paranoid, eventually cathartic. The prose should make readers feel the unnatural state of constant wakefulness.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about a pill that's obviously bad — it genuinely works for most people. The question is what we lose even when technology "works."

## Target Audience
Readers interested in near-future dystopia and workplace critique. Fans of Dave Eggers, George Orwell, and late-capitalism satire.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (14 chapters)
1. The endless day — Maya's life on the pills
2. The first dream — waking hallucinations begin
3. The choice — quitting, consequences
4. The underground — finding the Sleeper community
5. The economy — how Sleepers survive
6. The movement — those who resist consciously
7. The research — Maya investigates her symptoms
8. The cover-up — pharmaceutical company secrets
9. The evidence — proving they knew
10. The alliance — building a case
11. The stakes — corporate retaliation
12. The exposure — going public
13. The backlash — society divided
14. The right to rest — aftermath and change

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Fluorescent office whites, exhaustion grays, dream-state purples, rare peaceful blues
- Cover concept: A city that's lit 24 hours with one dark apartment, or an eye that won't close
- Typography: Corporate clean fonts becoming distorted
