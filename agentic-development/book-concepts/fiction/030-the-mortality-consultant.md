# The Mortality Consultant

## Genre
Dark Comedy / Literary Fiction / Existential Drama

## Concept

Jerome Baptiste has the world's strangest job: he's a Mortality Consultant. For a substantial fee, he helps wealthy clients design their own deaths. Not assisted suicide — death design. He works with the terminally ill who want control over their final moments: the setting, the timing, the witnesses, the last words, the legacy. He's an event planner for the inevitable.

Jerome is brilliant at his work — compassionate, detail-oriented, philosophical about endings. His clients die exactly as they wished, with meaning and dignity. He's helped hundreds find peace in their final moments.

But Jerome has a secret: he's been diagnosed with the same terminal illness that afflicts many of his clients. He has eighteen months, maybe less. And despite designing beautiful deaths for others, he has no idea how he wants his own life to end. He's spent so long focusing on others' mortality that he's never confronted his own.

As Jerome continues his work while his health declines, he must finally answer the question he helps others answer: What makes a death good? And beyond that: What makes a life good enough to deserve a good ending?

## Key Themes
- Death acceptance and death anxiety
- The difference between helping others and helping yourself
- Whether death can be "designed" or only accepted
- Legacy and what we leave behind
- The privilege of choosing your ending
- Finding meaning knowing the end is near

## Tone
Darkly funny, philosophically rich, ultimately moving. The prose should balance gallows humor with genuine tenderness.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about death as tragedy but death as the final creative act. The humor makes the profundity accessible.

## Target Audience
Readers of literary fiction with philosophical bent. Fans of Matt Haig, Fredrik Backman, and dark comedies. Adults thinking about mortality without wanting misery.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (12 chapters)
1. The consultation — Jerome meets a new client
2. The diagnosis — Jerome's own mortality revealed
3. A beautiful death — completing a client's design
4. The secret — hiding his illness from everyone
5. The failures — deaths that didn't go as planned
6. Jerome's past — how he became this
7. The question — what does he want for himself?
8. The difficult client — someone who challenges everything
9. Declining health — Jerome can't hide anymore
10. The apprentice — passing on his knowledge
11. The design — Jerome finally plans his own ending
12. The execution — whether the plan holds

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Elegant funeral blacks, living greens, warm golds, sunset oranges
- Cover concept: A man arranging flowers on his own grave, or a planner notebook with "The End" as the last entry
- Typography: Elegant but not morbid — life-affirming despite the subject
