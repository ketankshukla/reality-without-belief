# The Cartographer of Forgotten Places

## Genre
Fantasy / Literary Fiction / Magical Realism

## Concept

Ezra Thorne is a cartographer with an unusual specialty: he maps places that no longer exist. Demolished neighborhoods, razed forests, flooded valleys, burned cities — he reconstructs them on paper from interviews, photographs, and research, creating detailed maps of absence for clients who need to remember what was lost. It's melancholy work, but it pays well.

Then a woman hires him to map a place that shouldn't exist at all: a town called Morrow's End that appears on no historical records, yet she insists she grew up there. She has photographs, memories, a birth certificate — but every piece of evidence contradicts itself. The population changed every time she visited. The geography shifted. The town was simultaneously founded in 1842, 1903, and 1967.

As Ezra investigates, he discovers that Morrow's End is real — but it exists in the gaps between maps, in the spaces cartographers leave blank, in the places too strange to document. It's a sanctuary for the forgotten: people, buildings, entire communities that slipped through the cracks of history. And someone is hunting them, erasing them from the sanctuary too, leaving no trace they ever existed. Ezra must find and map the unmappable before Morrow's End disappears forever — taking his client's entire past with it.

## Key Themes
- Memory, documentation, and what makes something "real"
- The forgotten and overlooked in history
- The power of being seen and recorded
- Gentrification, displacement, and erasure
- The tension between preservation and change
- Finding belonging in liminal spaces

## Tone
Wistful, mysterious, intellectually playful. The prose should feel like old maps — detailed, slightly antiquated, full of unexpected discoveries. Wonder and melancholy intertwined.

## Unique Angle
This fantasy is grounded in real concerns about what gets preserved and what gets erased from history. The magical elements serve as metaphors for the very real disappearance of communities, cultures, and ways of life.

## Target Audience
Readers of literary fantasy and magical realism. Fans of Neil Gaiman, Erin Morgenstern, and Susanna Clarke. People interested in history, maps, and urban exploration. Adults 25-60.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (15 chapters)
1. The cartographer's trade — Ezra's unusual specialty
2. The impossible commission — the woman with contradictory memories
3. Research begins — evidence that makes no sense
4. The blank spaces — what maps leave out
5. First glimpse — Ezra sees Morrow's End briefly
6. The forgotten — meeting residents of the unmapped town
7. The sanctuary's rules — how the place works
8. The hunter — someone is erasing the erased
9. Mapping the unmappable — Ezra's impossible task
10. The woman's truth — her full story revealed
11. Who decides what's real — the philosophy of documentation
12. The pattern — understanding what the hunter wants
13. The final map — Ezra's masterwork
14. Confrontation — battle for Morrow's End
15. Recorded — aftermath and preservation

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Aged parchment, cartographic browns and greens, compass rose gold, faded ink blues
- Cover concept: An antique map with a section dissolving or revealing a hidden town, or a compass pointing to a place that isn't labeled
- Typography: Elegant serif with cartographic flourishes, perhaps with hand-drawn map elements
