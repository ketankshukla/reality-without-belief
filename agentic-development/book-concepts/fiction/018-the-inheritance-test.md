# The Inheritance Test

## Genre
Literary Thriller / Family Drama / Social Commentary

## Concept

When billionaire patriarch Victor Harrington dies, his will contains an unusual stipulation: his $4 billion fortune will go entirely to whichever of his four adult children can prove they have "contributed most meaningfully to human welfare" over the next twelve months. An independent panel of ethicists, philanthropists, and community leaders will judge. The children — none of whom have ever had to work for anything — must suddenly demonstrate genuine virtue.

The eldest, Marcus, was a hedge fund manager who destroyed a pension fund. The second, Diana, runs a lifestyle brand built on underpaid labor. The third, Theodore, has spent a decade in and out of rehab. The youngest, Celeste, is a social media influencer famous for nothing. Each sees the inheritance as rightfully theirs, and each begins a frantic campaign of visible do-gooding — founding charities, funding schools, volunteering conspicuously.

But Victor's true game becomes clear as the year progresses: he designed the test not to identify which child is best, but to expose what each of them truly values. The judgment panel is secretly documenting not their public acts but their private choices. And one of the children, slowly understanding the real test, must decide whether to play the game honestly and lose, or game the system like their father taught them and win.

## Key Themes
- The performance of virtue vs. genuine goodness
- Inherited wealth and its moral implications
- Whether character can be changed under pressure
- Family dysfunction and its roots
- The gap between public reputation and private behavior
- Whether redemption is possible for the privileged

## Tone
Sharp, satirical, but with genuine emotional stakes. The prose should balance dark comedy about wealth with sincere exploration of whether any of these people can actually change.

## Unique Angle
This isn't about finding out which child "wins" — it's about what the competition reveals about each of them, and whether the act of trying to be good can actually make someone good.

## Target Audience
Readers of literary fiction with thriller elements. Fans of Succession (TV), Kevin Kwan, and Donna Tartt. Adults interested in wealth, ethics, and family dynamics.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (16 chapters, rotating POV)
1. The reading — the will's unusual terms
2. Marcus: The calculation — how to win efficiently
3. Diana: The rebrand — virtue as marketing
4. Theodore: The chance — maybe this saves him
5. Celeste: The content — turning charity into views
6. The first quarter — public scorecards
7. Marcus: The shortcut — discovering he can buy outcomes
8. Diana: The workers — confronting her supply chain
9. Theodore: The relapse — crisis mid-competition
10. Celeste: The real — accidentally doing genuine good
11. The panel's secret — what they're actually watching
12. Alliances and betrayals — siblings conspire
13. The investigation — one child discovers the true test
14. Victor's ghost — understanding what their father wanted
15. Final acts — each child's closing argument
16. The judgment — who wins, and what it means

## Visual Identity Suggestions
- Color palette: Old money golds, legal document creams, privileged greens, blood red
- Cover concept: A gold key on velvet with cracks visible, or four silhouettes reaching for a crown
- Typography: Elegant, almost pretentious serif — old wealth aesthetic
