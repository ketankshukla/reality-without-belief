# The Information Diet

## Genre
Digital Wellness / Productivity / Cultural Criticism

## Concept

Information has become toxic in the same way food became toxic: abundance without nutrition. We're consuming more information than any generation in history while becoming less informed. This book diagnoses information obesity and prescribes an information diet.

The problem isn't that you don't know enough — it's that you know too much of the wrong things. News cycles, social media, hot takes, and content optimized for engagement rather than understanding are making you dumber, more anxious, and less capable of good judgment.

This book provides a framework for evaluating information quality, designing an information environment that nourishes rather than depletes, and building the discipline to consume information intentionally rather than reactively.

## Key Themes
- Information as nutrition (quality matters)
- The cost of low-quality information
- Designing information environments
- Slow information vs. fast information
- The discipline of not-knowing
- Building information taste

## Tone
Critical but not preachy, practical, research-backed. The author has been through information overload.

## Unique Angle
Treating information consumption with the same seriousness as food consumption — complete with nutritional frameworks.

## Target Audience
News addicts, doomscrollers, anyone who feels worse after consuming information. Knowledge workers drowning in content.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (10 chapters)
1. Information Obesity — the epidemic
2. Information Nutrition — what makes content nourishing
3. The Junk Information Index — recognizing garbage
4. The Audit — examining your current diet
5. Slow Information — sources that age well
6. The Elimination Phase — cutting the obvious toxins
7. Curated Consumption — designing your inputs
8. The Discipline of Ignorance — choosing not to know
9. Information Taste — developing discernment
10. The Informed Life — what real knowledge feels like
