# The Case Against Passion

## Genre
Career / Self-Development / Cultural Criticism

## Concept

"Follow your passion" might be the worst career advice ever given. This book argues that the passion hypothesis — the idea that the key to career satisfaction is finding work you're passionate about — has created a generation of anxious, job-hopping professionals who are constantly searching for a feeling that work isn't designed to provide.

Drawing on research and interviews with fulfilled professionals, this book shows that passion usually follows mastery, not the other way around. The people who love their work didn't find their passion — they built it through developing rare and valuable skills, gaining autonomy, and creating work that matters.

The book provides an alternative framework for career satisfaction: focus not on finding work you love but on becoming so good at something that the love follows. It's a practical guide for anyone who's exhausted by the passion search and ready for a more realistic approach to work satisfaction.

## Key Themes
- The passion trap and its costs
- How passion actually develops (after mastery)
- Skills as career capital
- Autonomy and its role in satisfaction
- Mission and meaning in work
- Building a career vs. finding one

## Tone
Evidence-based, practical, slightly contrarian. Challenging conventional wisdom with data and logic.

## Unique Angle
Unlike books that help you "find your passion," this one argues you should stop looking and start building.

## Target Audience
Young professionals paralyzed by career indecision. Mid-career workers who've followed passion and found disappointment. Anyone skeptical of the passion gospel.
