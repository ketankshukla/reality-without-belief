# The Good Enough Parent

## Genre
Parenting / Psychology / Self-Development

## Concept

Modern parenting culture has convinced us that anything less than optimization is failure. Every decision feels high-stakes. Every mistake seems permanent. Parents are more anxious than ever — and ironically, this anxiety may be harming kids more than the "mistakes" it's trying to prevent.

This book makes the case for "good enough" parenting — the psychologically-grounded concept that children don't need perfect parents, they need present, consistent, loving-enough parents. It challenges the perfectionism epidemic in modern parenting and provides permission to be adequate.

The paradox: trying to be a perfect parent often makes you a worse one, because anxious, burned-out parents aren't actually present. The good enough parent is available, and that matters more than optimization.

## Key Themes
- The perfectionism trap in parenting
- "Good enough" as psychological concept
- What children actually need (less than you think)
- Parental anxiety and its transmission to kids
- Permission to be adequate
- Presence over performance

## Tone
Reassuring, evidence-based, permission-giving. The author is a parent who's also struggled with this.

## Unique Angle
Not another optimization book but an argument for sustainable, adequate parenting.

## Target Audience
Anxious parents, especially first-time parents. Parents burned out by perfectionism. Those who suspect they're overthinking it but need permission to back off.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (10 chapters)
1. The Perfect Parent Myth — how we got here
2. What Kids Actually Need — research on requirements
3. The Good Enough Concept — psychological origins
4. Parental Anxiety — how it harms more than mistakes do
5. The Optimization Trap — diminishing returns
6. Presence Over Performance — being there vs. doing perfectly
7. Sustainable Parenting — avoiding burnout
8. Common "Mistakes" That Don't Matter — permission list
9. What Actually Does Matter — the essentials
10. The Good Enough Life — parenting without perfectionism
