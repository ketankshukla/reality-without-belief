# The Death of Expertise

## Genre
Cultural Criticism / Epistemology / Social Commentary

## Concept

We live in an age where everyone's opinion is considered equally valid, where "doing your own research" means watching YouTube videos, and where credentialed experts are dismissed as "elites" while random internet voices are trusted. This book explores how we got here and what it's costing us.

The death of expertise isn't about experts being wrong (they sometimes are) — it's about the collapse of any shared framework for evaluating claims. When a doctor's opinion carries no more weight than a celebrity's tweet, when decades of study are dismissed with "well, that's just your opinion," something fundamental has broken in how we process information together.

This book examines the social, technological, and psychological factors that created this crisis, and offers a framework for rebuilding trust in legitimate expertise while maintaining healthy skepticism of authority.

## Key Themes
- The collapse of epistemic authority
- How social media democratized misinformation
- The difference between skepticism and denial
- Why expertise matters (and its limits)
- Rebuilding trust in knowledge institutions
- Citizen responsibility in the information age

## Tone
Frustrated but constructive, evidence-based, accessible. The author is alarmed but not elitist.

## Unique Angle
This isn't a defense of experts being always right — it's an argument for having shared standards for evaluating claims at all.

## Target Audience
Educated adults concerned about misinformation. Teachers, doctors, scientists frustrated by dismissal. Anyone navigating a post-truth environment.
