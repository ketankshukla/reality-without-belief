# The Time Poverty Cure

## Genre
Productivity / Self-Development / Cultural Criticism

## Concept

Everyone is busy. No one has time. Yet we have more labor-saving devices, more efficiency tools, and more automation than any generation in history. Where did the time go?

This book diagnoses time poverty — the subjective experience of never having enough time — and provides structural solutions. The problem isn't that you need better time management; it's that modern life has been designed to consume all available time, no matter how much you optimize.

The cure requires not just individual tactics but a fundamental rethinking of your relationship with time, including questioning the premise that all time should be productive.

## Key Themes
- Time poverty as epidemic
- Why efficiency hasn't helped
- The time-consuming design of modern life
- Structural vs. individual solutions
- Time affluence as goal
- Reclaiming unscheduled time

## Tone
Analytical, practical, slightly radical. The author questions the assumptions of time management.

## Unique Angle
Not more optimization but questioning why we need so much optimization — structural analysis.

## Target Audience
Chronically busy people who've tried time management. Those who sense something is wrong with the whole framework. Anyone who wants time, not just productivity.

## Suggested Chapter Structure (10 chapters)
1. The Time Famine — everyone is busy
2. The Paradox — more efficiency, less time
